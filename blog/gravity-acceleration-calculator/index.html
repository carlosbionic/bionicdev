We all heard at least once or twice the word gravity. But what is gravity? In this article I am going to talk about the acceleration of gravity near the surface of Earth (or any other celestial body).

Pick up some random object (a tennis ball can be a good example; please don't do this with your cell phone...). Lift it above the floor about 1.5 or 2 meters. Drop it. What happened? The tennis ball started to fall increasing the velocity over time. When velocity changes over time we say there is an acceleration. In this case, the acceleration is present because there is a force, which is called the force of gravity.

This force of gravity is the one that, for example, keeps the Moon orbiting around the Earth, and the Earth orbiting around the Sun.

In this article we are going to concentrate on an object near the surface of a big celestial body like the Earth, and compute the acceleration when the object falls, namely the acceleration of gravity (sometimes called just gravity).

Gravity acceleration

Let's begin with Newton's second law of dynamics:

\[ F = m\cdot a \]

Force equals mass times acceleration. By Force I mean the sum of all the forces acting on the body of mass m. Figure 2 shows the system corresponding to a celestial body of mass M and a small body of mass m. The mass m for our example would be the small tennis ball.

In this case there will be a unique force acting between the two bodies, namely the gravitational force. We know that this force strongly depends on the distance between the center of mass of both bodies. This distance can be expressed as the sum of the radius R of the big celestial body, and the height h of the small body measured from the surface. We are assuming that the small body's dimensions are insignificant with respect to the big body.

The gravitational force between the bodies of mass M and m separated by a distance r can be expressed as:

\[ F_G = G\cdot\frac{m\cdot M}{r^2} \]

In this equation, G is a constant, m and M the masses and r the distance between the two bodies. G is called Gravitational constant [1]. It has a value of 6.67428e-11 m3kg-1s-2.

Replacing Newton's second law for the left hand side (let's call g the acceleration, since we are talking about the acceleration of gravity):

\[ m\cdot g = G\cdot\frac{m\cdot M}{r^2} \] \[ g = \frac{G\cdot M}{r^2} \]

In the last step, the mass m cancels out. This is the proof that any two bodies fall with the same acceleration near the surface of the Earth, independently of the masses. This is true only if we neglect the air drag. If you compare a tennis ball and a piece of paper falling to the ground, they will likely fall with very different accelerations because the air drag is bigger for the paper.

Lastly, we can express the distance r as the sum of R and h (see Figure 2), and the expression for the gravity is:

\[ g = \frac{G\cdot M}{(R+h)^2} \]

This is the equation used in the Gravity calculator below. As we can see, the value of the gravity depends on the height, which is measured from the planet's surface (celestial body). The value of the gravity decreases as the height increases.

Here is a summary of what each parameter means:

  • g: the acceleration due to gravity (m/s2)
  • G: gravity constant. In this article we will use a value of 6.67428e-11 m3kg-1s-2
  • M: mass of the celestial body (Kg)
  • R: radius of the celestial body (Km)
  • h: height (m, Km). This is the distance from the planet's surface to the point where the gravity is to be computed.
